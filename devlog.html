Development Log

Engineering journal

A running record of the software, hardware, and simulation work behind LEGOSwarm — coordination experiments, manipulation and perception tests, and the failures in between. This log is the long-term archive of how the platform gets built.

June 8, 2026

A worker that really builds

The first end-to-end run worked, but it wasn’t pretty — the robot tipped, shoved parts around, and rarely finished cleanly. This update is about making it reliable, and the biggest culprit was hiding in plain sight: the worker was navigating by wheel odometry, which quietly drifts, so it kept driving to almost-the-right place and just missing. We switched it to navigate against ground-truth position — a stand-in for the localization a real robot will eventually run on its own — and the accuracy snapped into place. From there it was honest engineering: giving the drive base enough torque to actually turn, holding each part rigidly to the gripper through the carry instead of letting it flash or fling across the arena, and spacing the supply parts and build targets so the robot never trips over its own work. The result is a worker that drives out to parts scattered around the arena, carries each one cleanly, and lays them down in a tidy row — placing within a few millimeters of target, on its own, from start to finish.

June 4, 2026

First build, in simulation

The full pipeline ran end-to-end for the first time. In a Gazebo physics simulation, a single worker reads its next job from the shared build plan, drives to the part, picks it, places it, and then checks its own work against ground truth before declaring the step done. It is not clean yet — early runs tipped the robot over and shoved parts around on retries, and we are still tuning the arm and grasp for a reliably repeatable “complete.” But the loop is closed: intent in, verified placement out, with no human in the loop.

June 3, 2026

Teaching one robot to build

Before a swarm can build anything, one robot has to. We built the worker engine inside WEAVE (Workload Execution and Autonomous Verification Engine) — the layer that turns a single job into motion. Jobs compile down through reusable “recipes” into behavior trees, with recovery and verification built in at every step: a placement is never marked complete until it has been independently verified, otherwise the job is handed back to the swarm. A deliberate seam separates the robot’s decision-making — deterministic, and fully testable on a laptop — from the physical acting, so the same logic drives a stub, a simulator, or real hardware unchanged.

June 2, 2026

A swarm with no boss

Most multi-robot systems lean on a central scheduler handing out assignments. WEAVE has none. Every robot carries its own live copy of the build plan, and the copies stay in agreement purely by exchanging small messages — no dispatcher, no broker, no polling. Robots claim work with short-lived “leases” that expire on their own, so if a robot vanishes mid-task its work automatically reopens for someone else, with no central health-monitor noticing. We found a way to make the whole swarm agree on who-does-what at a cost of only a couple of messages per job; the comparison against a traditional centralized baseline is one of the experiments we are running.

May 30, 2026

The blueprint is the brain

WEAVE starts by turning a real LEGO instruction booklet into something a swarm can reason about. A vision-language model reads the booklet page by page, and each assembly step becomes a node in a dependency graph — what part, where it goes, and what must exist before it. Parts are grounded against a real catalog so the plan refers to canonical components, not guesses. The result is a single living graph that is simultaneously the blueprint, the task list, and the progress tracker — the shared source of truth every robot works from.

May 26, 2026

Bring-up & first 3D-printed parts

Starting code is in place and we are experimenting with the 3D printer to fabricate the first worker chassis and gripper iterations. Early effort is focused on the mechanical platform and the perception scaffolding needed for part detection.

Planned

Multi-robot decentralized build

Scaling from one worker to many: several robots pulling and claiming jobs from their own replicated build plans and assembling concurrently, with congestion-aware navigation and coalitions for jobs too large for a single arm. We will measure how the swarm holds up as robot count and structure complexity increase, against a centralized baseline.
